Scotland: 2024 Euro Winners

By Asad

To many, Scotland is a land of misbehaving drunkards, who live in clans, wear kilts, guzzle Scotch, and speak in thick accents. The country is cold, dark, and unforgiving. The football is similarly abject: the national team has been crap for ages, and Scottish sides regularly get boyed off in Europe. All in all, Scotland seems grim.

However, if you dig deeper, you’ll see that Scotland’s not what its detractors—mainly the English—have made it out to be. It’s a belting place, with a rich legacy of culture and prestige. Also, Scotland’s clubs were once able to go toe-to-toe with Europe’s top sides. Unfortunately, recent predicaments—poverty, homelessness, mass emigration, and poor football—sully its image.

What is Scotland’s legacy? Why has the country been in perpetual decline? How did Scottish football go from Jimmy Johnstone, Billy Bremner, and Denis Law, to Oliver McBurnie and Grant Hanley? And, despite this drastic decline, why do I still back the Scots to make a run in the 2024 European Championship?

Law winning the 1964 Ballon D'or.

In this article, I’ll shine some light on Scotland’s greatness. The Scots have done bits for the British Empire, various developing countries, and world football. They’re an understated, proud group of people. However, this isn’t charity: the Scots can fend for themselves, as history has proven. Rather, it’s an ode to them. God bless the Scots, Scotland, and Scottish football.

Early History (10,800 BC - 1025 AD)

To start, let’s look at Scotland’s makeup. As alluded to earlier, Scotland is a dark, cold place for most of the year. However, it wasn’t always like this. In fact, for the majority of history, it was worse. During the Last Ice Age, Scotland had temperatures of negative 60 degrees Celsius, and its land was covered by a sheet of ice that was around a mile-deep. Even after the ice had melted, Scotland remained a frigid tundra, and few were able to create permanent settlements.

But, during the Classical Era, various groups established themselves all across Scotland. The Britons, a Welsh-Celtic group, settled on the Clyde. The Picts—a mysterious group of warriors, likely coming from northern Germany—lived far north, beyond the Forth and Clyde. Little is known about them, aside from the fact that they weren’t Gaelic. But, we do know that they were far more aggressive than the Britons, as they regularly launched raids into the south. Additionally, the Gaelic Scots settled in the west, creating the Gaelic kingdom of Dalriada. Many came from Ireland, but the Scots were distinct from the Irish, as much of their culture was home-grown and specific to their needs as Scotsmen.

These groups were formidable. After Rome conquered most of Britain in the early 1st century AD, they had a go at the north. The Britons struggled, eventually ceding portions of land. But, the Picts and the Scots endured the Roman advances and counter-raided on numerous occasions. Eventually, the message got through. Roman Emperor Hadrian built his famous wall in 122 AD to consolidate Rome’s southern possessions (e.g. England), as these northern groups were far too resillient.

Where Hadrian's Wall was.

However, these groups also fought amongst each other. They’d form alliances to fight off enemies, break these alliances to fight one another, and then recreate these alliances to fight new enemies. As a result, for centuries, power oscillated amongst the Picts, the Gaelic Scots, and the emerging Northumbrian English. It was chaos. Order only came in the mid-9th century, when Scottish leader Cináed mac Ailpín united the Picts and Scots to form the Kingdom of Alba, or Scotland. This formalised a singular Scottish identity across northern Britain, albeit centuries of inter-marriage and inter-settlement had laid the seeds.

The unification came just in time. The Vikings began raiding, and a unified force stood the best chance at resisting. From about 800 to 1050 AD, the Vikings sailed off to various foreign lands—like Scotland, Russia, England, and Ireland—and relentlessly pillaged everything in sight, demolishing villages and enslaving many. These chaps were steely and ruthless.

The Vikings came in on long-boats and did damage.

However, so were the newly-unified Scots. Whilst Viking raids were frequent, Scottish groups resisted. Much like the Romans, the Vikings found it difficult to breach the Scots’ defences. They were able to plunder the Island of Iona and destroy a few monasteries, but true Viking settlement and damage was limited to the islands—e.g. the Isle of Man and Iona—and the far north.

The English, however, struggled, ultimately letting the Vikings conquer ¾ of its kingdoms after fifteen years of conquest. By the 11th century, the majority of England was ruled by Vikings, after successful expeditions by Sweyn Forkbeard and Cnut the Great. In Ireland, the Vikings destroyed most monasteries and enslaved many. These newly-enslaved men were then sent to Dublin, where they were sold to the Moors.

As the Kingdom of Alba entered the 2nd millennium, it was a uniquely Gaelic realm, populated by no-nonsense, gritty men. Although invaders—e.g. the Romans, Vikings and Northumbrian English—did damage, the damage paled in comparison to that done to the English and the Irish. Additionally, whilst the Anglos, Jutes, and the Saxons dominated England during the 9th century, they struggled to assert themselves in the Kingdom of Alba. These disparities put the Scots in a unique position, strengthening their culture and identity.

Middle History (1025 AD - 1700 AD)

As these lads entered the later stages of the Middle Ages, external influences crept in. The Normans, who were prominent in England, expanded their influence. After William the Conqueror became England’s first Norman king in 1066, Norman settlements spread across England and, to a lesser extent, Scotland. King David I of Scotland (1124-1153) promoted Norman settlement by offering land and financial incentives.

In the 11th century, the Normans invaded and occupied England. Some came to Scotland.

As a result, a sizable Anglo-Norman contingency grew, especially in Moray, Mearns, and Gowrie. As they did in England, they promoted feudal landholding systems, which were adopted all across the Lowlands. As a result, Scotland began to Anglicise, albeit notable distinctions remained, especially in the Highlands. The Highlanders resisted feudalism and stayed true to their Gaelic Scottish roots, even as James II and James V sought to impose their rule. Clans, kinship, and chieftains remained. Scottish kings, realising the futility of compulsion, were forced to negotiate with earls in the Highlands and the Islands to maintain their influence.

Furthermore, as the Kingdoms of England and Scotland strengthened, their relationship became more strained. From the 11th century onwards, the Scots and the English quarreled over spheres of influence. Certain Scottish kings had peaceful relations with England—such as Alexander III—but others—like Malcolm III—were more bloodthirsty, frequently raiding England’s northern posessions, like Northumberland and Carlisle.

The Kingdoms’ kings had oscillating relationships. Certain kings were more violent than others. In 1173, Henry II made William the Lion his vassal and forced him to give up castles in Edinburgh and Sterling. In 1296, John Balliol surrendered Scotland to the English, allowing them to tax and patrol the Scots. However, thanks to the efforts of William Wallace and Robert I, Scotland regained its independence in March 1328. Later, Robert I’s son married the daughter of Edward III, the English king, which reduced quarrels. Afterwards, the likes of Henry IV occasionally invaded Scotland, but the Scots endured these invasions, and Scotland remained independent.

In the end, English meddling helped the Scots cement their unique identity. In the 1320 Declaration of Arbroath, written to persuade the pope to denounce England’s subjugation of Scotland, Robert I portrayed the Scots as a proud people, who were able to resist the Danes, English, and Norse, and who had the right to govern themselves. It didn’t work, but it strengthened the Scottish resolve, and they promptly won independence in 1328.

Robert the Bruce, a Scottish national hero. He liberated Scotland from English subjugation.

Eventually, with James VI’s ascension to the English crown in 1603, England and Scotland were, on paper, united, marking the Union of the Crowns. It didn’t end the tensions between the Scots and the English. It also did not stop internal discontent, as seen by the regular clashes between the Jacobite Highlanders and the Anglicised Lowlanders. But, the countries were mostly stable, and the 17th century saw an increase in productive relations.

Golden Era (1700 AD - 1914 AD)

However, as the 18th century approached, Scotland’s situation was dire. The Scots, having seen the success of the British East India Company, created their own imitation—the Darien Company—in hopes of replicating its glory by colonising Panama. The Darien Company raised around £400k, much of which came from the Scottish government. Unfortunately, the expeditions, which took place in the 1690s, failed. Famine, disease, and poor yields were rife, and the Scottish economy was bankrupted. Concurrently, there were poor harvests back in Scotland, causing many to starve. Scotland’s grave predicament, as well as well-timed bribes by the English, led to the Union of 1707. Thus, England absorbed Scotland, and Great Britain was born.

Trade

But, the Union was a blessing. Although most of Scotland’s autonomy was lost—for example, Scottish Parliament disappeared—Scotland’s ties to the affluent England created avenues of trade. According to Tom Devine, in 1758, “Scottish tobacco imports” from America were greater than those of “London, Bristol, Liverpool, and Whitehaven” combined. Ireland, however, had little to no stake in the prospering American markets. The English passed the Act of 1696, which banned most American imports. As a result, both England and Scotland had quasi-monopolies over American trade.

Tobacco was a massive industry in America, and the Scots profited from it.

Similarly, the Scots dominated the West Indian sugar trade. “Sugar princes” became millionaires, and organisations, such as Alexander Houstoun & Company, surged.b Scotland’s economy was on the rise, and Glasgow, Clyde, Edinburgh, and other cities benefitted.

Innovation

Beyond a booming economy, Scotland also became a hub of innovation. The Scots always emphasised the importance of reading and education, as they believed that people needed to read the Bible. The University of St. Andrews, for example, was founded early—in 1413—and was immensely cheaper than Oxford University and Cambridge University, meaning you didn’t need to be posh to be literate. University of Glasgow and Edinburgh University followed in 1451 and 1583 respectively. Further, the Calvinist belief in doing your best work—all the time, for the glory of God—ushered the Scots towards self-improvement, mainly via education.

With educated upper and middle classes, along with its booming economy, Scotland grew. Edinburgh and Glasgow, in particular, prospered. Their populations surged—especially in the midst of the Industrial Revolution—which increased urban development, employment, and immigration. Eventually, immigration forced Edinburgh to expand, so the Edinburgh Town Council built the northern, Georgian New Town, which was connected to the Old Town via the North Bridge. The bridge still stands, adding to Edinburgh’s distinctive charm.

Edinburgh's lovely North Bridge.

As a result of their growth, these cities became melting pots for ideas. Many Scottish Enlightenment thinkers—such as David Hume, William Robertson, and William Ferugsson—lived close to one another, often crossing paths. This drove the Enlightenment, as these chaps were able to draw inspiration from and bounce ideas off one another. Such interactions elevated the likes of Adam Smith, Francis Hutcheson, Henry Peter Brougham, and Henry Home to higher levels of understanding. As a result, Edinburgh and Glasgow gained lofty reputations, prompting Voltaire to remark that “it is to Scotland that we look for our idea of civilisation.”

The rest of Scotland developed, too, with new roads and bridges connecting the isolated Highlands to the rest of Scottish society. The Caledonian Canal, for example, opened the Highlands to commercial traffic. Despite the Highland clearances, the Scots began taking more interest in their Highlander heritage. Even the likes of Adam Smith and Adam Ferguson—who, respectively, founded the modern fields of economics and sociology—saw the value of the Highlanders. In particular, Smith appreciated the health and courage benefits associated with Highlander-esque militias. Coupled with the influence of Walter Scott, whose work about the Highlands increased interest, Scotland’s tourism industry boomed.

Impact on America

Scottish impact extended beyond Scotland. For centuries, Protestant countries—like Norway and Sweden—relied on Highlander military support. For example, in the 17th century, Gustavus Adolphus recruited the military services of the Highlander Clan Munro, whose power helped transform Sweden into a global superpower. Further, Scots were involved in the Rotterdam stock exchange, founded in 1598.

But, during the Industrial Age, Scottish impact multiplied. As alluded to earlier, the Scots moulded America. From between 1717 to 1776, around 250,000 Ulstermen came to America, most of whom had Scottish backgrounds. Also, throughout the 18th and 19th centuries, around 3 million Scots emigrated to all sorts, mainly America. Similar to the Irish, the Scots descended upon the United States, bringing valuable skills and knowledge to the fledging country. The civil engineering expertise of the Clyde shipbuilders, for example, was crucial to the development of American engineering, and the doctors, politicians, etc. all modernised the country.

The Clyde shipyards.

One of Scotland’s most important influences, though, was education. Many American universities—like Princeton, William & Mary, Columbia, and Brown—were influenced by Scottish education. In particular, Princeton had a deep connection to the Scots, having been (partially) founded by Ulsterman Samuel Blair. Many of its early presidents were Scottish. One of them, John Witherspoon, modelled its curriculum off Edinburgh University. Later, James McCosh founded the graduate school and schools of science, and erected numerous buildings around campus.

Scottish investment was also omnipresent. The Scottish government invested millions into American railroads. In addition, Scottish entrepreneurs boosted the United States’s economy. Andrew Carnegie, for example, founded the Carnegie Steel Company and employed tens-of-thousands. When J.P. Morgan bought the company in the early 20th century, Carnegie became one of the richest men ever and proceeded to construct pools, auditoriums, libraries, etc., all over the country.

All in all, the Scots were key to America’s development. Whilst many were proud of their Scottish heritage, they quickly assimilated and attached to the American identity, partially why many are unaware of Scotland’s impact on America. The Irish, on the other hand, maintained their links to Ireland, establishing large Irish communities (e.g. Philadelphia and Boston) and bringing over Irish politics, as seen by the Orange Riots of 1870 and 1871. Coupled with their poor living conditions—many lived in ghettoes—and lack of education, anti-Irish sentiment was, unfortunately, rife. The mistreatment of the Irish in the North was even used by Southerners to defend slavery, as they argued that slaves were treated better in the South.

Empire Builders

Beyond America, the Scots were crucial for the British Empire, helping consolidate its holdings. After the Revolutionary War, the British were weak. Despite incredible naval power, and the deployment of Highlanders, they’d lost their richest colony. Further, British loyalists were bombed-out of the United States, fleeing to Canada. All in all, they looked like jokes. However, 30 years after the Revolutionary War, Britain became Europe’s most powerful state. How did Britain undergo such a remarkable turnaround? You know the answer.

After losing America, the Brits consolidated their holdings in Canada. The Hudson's Bay Company (HBC) and the Northwest Company (NWC) heightened their dominance over the fur trade. The HBC—run by Sir George Simpson, a Highlander from Ross-shire—grew to trade across “3 million square miles of territory.” Incredible. The HBC and the NWC employed loads of Scots, often from the Orkney Islands, as these chaps were gritty enough to survive in Canada’s unforgiving cold. They also employed the services of the loyalist Highlanders, who, again, pissed off out of the United States. With their influence, these companies helped turn Montreal and Halifax into prosperous trading centres.

Orkneymen, sailing off to Canada.

Also, Scottish muscle helped the British defeat the French in Trafalgar and Waterloo. By finishing the belligerent Napoleon, the Empire cemented itself as Europe’s strongest state. Further victories—such as in the 1840 Opium War, a war started by Scottish opium traders—were backed by Scottish muscle and ingenuity, with John Laird’s iron ships playing a key role in demolishing opposition.

After these battles, Scottish regiments, like the 42nd (Royal Highland) Regiment, received heroes’ welcomes. To commemorate their bravery, citizens created graffiti, songs, and symbols. However, this was nothing new. For example, in Ulster, a country comprised of ethnic Scots, people had been memoralising the Siege of Derry for years. In later years, the 36th (Ulster) Division’s efforts in Somme were treated similarly. All in all, the Scots are a proud people. It is imperative to stand up for what’s right, make sacrifices, and fight back against oppressors, as the Romans and Vikings can attest.

Graffiti that commemorates the Battle of Somme.

Further, the Scots helped urbanise Australia. Originally, Australia was a country dominated by uncouth, violent prisoners, but the Scots helped modernise it. One of Australia’s early politicians, the Hebridean Lachlan Macquarie, built schools, roads, and churches, which boosted education, development, and prosperity. Further, as the Empire accumulated more-and-more land, sheep farming became a massive industry, with Scottish entrepreneurs cashing in. Unfortunately, much of this land was at the expense of the Aboriginals, who were massacred by disease and hostilities.

Scottish influence was largely positive, though, and Scots migrated with good intentions. By the early 1800s, they started exploring Africa. Expeditions were spurred on by ministers, like Thomas Chalmers, who urged people to spread Christianity for philanthropic purposes. Macgregor Laird and David Livingstone were receptive to this call-to-arms and went off on expeditions. Few had explored central and western Africa before, as many believed it to be a disease-ridden, violent place, but Livingstone proved them wrong. He spent decades in Africa, fighting tigers, malaria, and (initially) hostile populations.

His persistence paid off, and he assimilated. He converted many to Christianity and worked to modernize towns. After spending time in African communities, he came to despise the slave trade. To protect against Arabic slave traders, he armed African chiefs and encouraged trade among the chiefdoms to reduce their reliance on the slave trade. However, in 1873, he succumbed to malaria.

His legacy influenced the European “Scramble for Africa” in the 19th century. But, many Africans appreciated his efforts. Zambian president Kenneth Kaunda called him the “first freedom fighter,” and various African cities have kept the names he gave them—such as Malawi’s Livingstonia and Blantyre, and Zambia’s Livingstone.

Livingstone's statue in Zimbabwe.

Decline and Modern Period (1914 AD - Present)

However, Scotland began declining after World War I, as centuries of emigration dwindled its pool of skilled workers, hurting domestic industries. Despite progress via the National Services Scotland (NHS), television, and improved housing, low wages drove emigration to places like Australia, England, Jamaica, and, surprisingly, Eastern Europe, where Scots have an interesting history—from 1691 to 1703, Aberdonian Alexander Chalmers served four terms as mayor of Warsaw. Between 1951 and 1981, over 753,000 Scots emigrated, nearly half of them to England.

Further, Scotland’s domestic industries took a nosedive, and not just because of the dwindling workforce. Barring temporary rises during World War 1 & 2, the global demand for jute, coal, and ships declined, hurting places like Clyde and Dundee. The increase in tertiary work—the services sector—also propelled the decline, hurting the agricultural and industrial industries. This also hurt Yugoslavia, contributing to its violent break-up.

After World War II, Scotland shifted away from traditional industries, leading to widespread factory closures and job losses. Labour governments stepped in to ease this transition, working with unions and providing grants to companies like Chrysler UK and Upper Clyde Shipbuilders. Their goal was to safeguard the economic security of working-class families, aiming to reduce the impact of industrial decline on local communities and create new opportunities.

However, under the Conservative tutelage of Edward Heath and Margaret Thatcher, deindustrialisation accelerated. Edward Heath minimised state support for Upper Clyde Shipbuilders, and Thatcher notoriously waged war against unions and encouraged privatisation. Following closures in Timex plants, Dundee lost 1,675 industrial jobs. Also, after the closure of a massive car plant in Linwood in 1981, over 4,800 workers were made redundant. Within the span of a century, prospering cities had turned into bastions of unemployment and poverty.

Working men and women, protesting against rapid deindustrialisation.

The Linwood closure underscored a significant trend during Thatcher's neoliberal era: privatised companies shuttered and relocated, impacting thousands. By 1971, Glasgow's shipbuilding workforce had dwindled to around 11,000, just 10% of its Edwardian peak. Despite sit-ins and work-ins, like those at Clydeside in the '60s and '70s, deindustrialization persisted. Conservative support for deindustrialisation spurred the Scottish National Party's rise and the Conservative Party's decline, while Anglophobia grew, as London-based decision-makers (“faceless men”) became targets of resentment.

These days, poverty and unemployment persist. From 2014 to 2017, approximately one million Scots, including around 230,000 children, were living in poverty. Many, particularly in the Scottish Labour Party, attributed this to UK government cuts and placed the blame on the Tories. After a brief decline, child poverty has been rising in recent years. Although relative poverty had been decreasing since 1995, it started increasing again, reaching 24% in 2023, mainly due to the impacts of COVID-19.

As for what the near future holds: who knows. We’re living in strange, strange times, and change takes ages. But, the Scots have bounced back from worse, so I have faith in them. The Scots have far too much knowledge, steel, and spirit. If anyone can bring about such a drastic and positive change, it's the Scots. Eventually, they’ll be back.

Competition

This year, Scotland’s biggest competitors are England, Spain, Germany, and Portugal. Some would put Italy, Holland, and Belgium into that list, but I’m not going to. Whilst those sides have pockets of talent, I don’t rate their depth. For example, look at Belgium. They’ve got Kevin de Bruyne, a magician. But, simultaneously, they rely on Romelu Lukaku, a serial bottlejob, to lead their attack. He nullifies De Bruyne’s quality. Belgium have also lost incredible footballers—like Vertonghen, Alderweireld, Nainggolan, and Hazard—in recent years. As a result, I don’t rate their chances. They’re top-heavy.

A vandalised Romelu Lukaku mural, after he pushed for a move to Juventus.

But, top-heaviness is not always an issue. Sir Alex Ferguson once claimed that he could win the European Cup with Zinedine Zidane and 10 planks of wood. But De Bruyne is no Zidane. He doesn’t have Zizou’s grace. Obviously he’s a world-class footballer, but everything about him is mechanical: the dribbling, passing, etc. There is no rawness to his game. He’s like a robot: everything’s refined and manufactured; there are no rough edges, none of the imperfections that drew people to the likes of Thierry Henry, Günter Netzer, and George Best. You need some rawness to your game, a burst of spontaneity, or else you become predictable. Unfortunately, he has none.

De Bruyne and City are a perfect match. A manufactured player for a manufactured football club. There’s no glory in either of their accomplishments, as there’s no risk of jeopardy. The same way there’s no risk of City having an off-season, there’s no risk of De Bruyne cocking up a cross. It all means nothing. So, if Belgium win the Euros off the back of De Bruyne’s quality, it’ll mean nothing, so I don’t consider them a legitimate threat to Scotland.

As for Italy, I think they have decent depth. But, they’ve weakened since their 2021 triumph, with Jorginho, Chiesa, and Bonucci having declined; also, Chiellini has retired. These are big losses. With this much degradation, it’s hard to say Italy have a chance.

Finally, Holland have great players, such as Van Dijk, de Jong, and de Ligt. But, like Belgium, their attack is spearheaded by a dilettante: Memphis Depay. I know all about Memphis Depay. He exposed himself at Manchester United. Whilst at the club, instead of learning to beat a defender or pass, he sulked and clashed with the media. He showed no inclination towards improving or trying to make it work at United. Instead, he wanted to look flash. He quickly bought himself an all-black Mercedes G-Wagon, with “M7” emblazoned on its headrests. Nauseating.

Memphis Depay's flash G-Wagon.

When Cristiano Ronaldo had originally signed for United, he immediately multiplied his previous efforts, understanding that he still had a long way to go. His attitude was spot-on. Like Mikhail Gorbachev once said, “If you don’t move forward, sooner or later you begin to move backward." Depay moved backward. He was happy to take his foot off the accelerator, shift his priorities, and indulge in customised headrests. Hence, he was quickly bombed-out of United. Since then, he has only succeeded at one club: Lyon (where, still, he won no trophies). He was poor at Barcelona, and he’s hardly impressed for Atletico. I wish him the best, but he’s not the one to lead Holland to success.

Now, onto the top sides. England are strong. I think Kane, Bellingham, Foden, and Alexander-Arnold are world-class, and that Saka, Rashford, Rice, and Stones are great. Off squad quality alone, they are easily the favourites. However, when it comes to the Auld Rivalry, squad quality goes out the window. Emotions dominate, and the Scottish footballers accordingly raise their game. As a result, Scotland fair well against the Auld Enemy. Very rarely do Scotland get battered by England, albeit that 9-3 still lives in the memories of older fans. If the two sides meet, I reckon it’ll be a slugfest, with McTominay scoring the winner.

As for the Iberian Peninsula, Spain have a great squad. Oyarzabal, Rodri, Grimaldo, Carvajal, and Merino are all class. Spain won the Nations League in 2023 and dominated their Euro Qualifiers, even beating Scotland in Spain. However, the Scottish won the reverse fixture in Glasgow, with an inspired McTominay scoring a double. Another side that are good, but not miles ahead of Scotland. If the Scottish players’ attitudes are spot-on, then they have a great chance to beat Spain.

Portugal regularly field geriatrics and immobiles—Ronaldo, William Carvalho, Pepe, and Danilo Pereira being prime examples. It’s fine to field one or two—France had Giroud in 2018—but when you field five or six, it’s a big, big problem.

Part of Spain’s issue in 2014 was that they were too slow and stiff. Xavi, Iniesta, Casillas, Xabi Alonso, Busquets, David Villa were all incredible footballers, but in 2014, most of them were immobile. As a result, they got boyed off in the group stage. Against Holland, Robben had free rein to sprint around and take the piss. As a result, Holland won 5-1, and Spain was on an early flight home.

Robben rounding Casillas.

However, outside of their 1966 World Cup run and Euro 2016 victory, they've historically underperformed. France’s 2010 debacle pales in comparison to Portugal's 1986 World Cup disaster, and between 1970 and 1998, Portugal only made the World Cup once. While their current team is strong, history often repeats itself. And, with Roberto Martínez at the helm, it probably will.

Football's Decline

All in all, I don’t rate many international sides. I think that football has declined massively the last decade-and-a-half. For example, look at Italy’s 2006 World Cup squad. It’s filled with footballing royalty: Luca Toni, Fabio Cannavaro, Gennaro Gattuso, Alessandro Del Piero, Mauro Camoranesi, Filippo Inzaghi, Andrea Pirlo, Francesco Totti, Gianluigi Buffon. I don’t think any of Italy’s players today gets into that squad.

Italy's 2006 squad. What a side.

Further, Spain’s 2010 squad: Iker Casillas, Xabi Alonso, Xavi, Iniesta, Fernando Torres, Sergio Ramos, David Villa. David Silva and Juan Mata couldn’t even crack the bench for them. These days, those 2 would be Spain’s best players, albeit Rodri competes.

Why’s this? I think that the abolition of foreign player limits has damaged the game. Sorry—this isn’t entirely irrelevant, and it might come off as a diatribe—but I think the game’s declined. If asked to list the top international teams from pre-Bosman times (e.g. pre-1995), most would likely name the 1960 Soviet Union, 1954 Hungary, 1984 France, and 1970 Brazil teams, amongst others. What do these sides have in common? Most of their players, if not all, played in their domestic countries, and, most importantly, they thrived there.

Take the 1970 Brazilian squad for example. These days, Tostão, Jairzinho, Carlos Alberto, Gérson, and (especially) Pelé would not have spent the majority of their careers in Brazil. They would have all been snapped up by a Real Madrid or Barcelona when they were 17. For proof, look at Real’s recent history with Brazillians: Vinícius, Endrick, Rodrygo, and Reinier were all purchased as teenagers, for massive sums. If Endrick commanded €60m, a 16 year old Pelé would’ve commanded €200m.

Pelé celebrating his goal in the 1970 World Cup final. A big game player.

Vinícius and Rodrygo have done bits, but they are the exception. I think the game suffers when young players from smaller leagues prematurely join top teams. Depay would’ve been better off had he stayed another couple seasons at PSV, ameliorating his game and character, before entering the strange, cutthroat environment at Manchester United. Instead, he sunk at United, halted his development, and, relative to early expectations, has underperformed—albeit he’s still had a decent career. Moving to a new country—where one needs to adapt to different customs and a new language—is not easy. But, if you are mature and have a good footballing education under your belt, you stand a fighting chance.

Some, like Vinícius and Erling Haaland, have defied the odds, but they’re rare. For every Haaland, you get 20 Francisco Trincãos and 10 Renato Sancheses. But, I get why young players move. These days, modern football’s lopsided nature demolishes the possibility of a (relatively) obscure footballing country, like Scotland, Hungary, or Russia, dominating with a squad of domestic players. This is why young lads feel the need to uproot.

Back in the days, Hungarian sides regularly made runs into the UEFA Cup, European Cup, and Cup Winners’ Cup. The likes of Ferencváros, Videoton, and MTK Budapest were able to regularly put away the likes of Liverpool, Manchester United, and Juventus; Dinamo Kiev, Torpedo Moscow, and Dinamo Tbilisi (not Russian, but Soviet) also did bits. Nowadays, Manchester United, even with how crap they are, would blow all of these sides away. United’s squad is too good for them, as their financial firepower is unmatched. Even as poor as Antony is, he’d walk into Ferencváros’s and Torpedo Moscow’s sides.

The Leeds United squad celebrating after beating Ferencváros in a European final. Hard to imagine either of these sides making a European final these days.

Beyond the wealth gap, top teams act like vultures, raiding lesser-known clubs in countries like Russia, Sweden, and Hungary. Alexander Isak spent 11 years at Swedish club AIK, receiving significant resources and education, only for Dortmund to sign him the second he turned 17. After just two years, they offloaded him to Real Sociedad. It's wasteful and rude.

If a UEFA-enforced foreigner limit is reintroduced into leagues, you’d see less of this waste. The top clubs would be forced to develop domestic talent and dissuaded from raiding lesser clubs. For example: instead of of wasting £70m on Kalvin Phillips and Mateo Kovačić, City would’ve given Romeo Lavia a chance and maintained their ties to their local community. (And, with how those two have performed for Abu Dhabi’s Manchester City, and how Lavia was for Southampton, there’s a good chance City would’ve been better off.)

As a result, football’s quality would go up, and the international game would be in a better place. Mikel Merino and Joselu, Newcastle United rejects, wouldn’t be Spain’s best players; we’d see more David Silvas and Xabi Alonsos. Furthermore, smaller clubs and leagues would be able to compete again. We probably wouldn’t see the likes of Panathinaikos or Malmö FF make the Champions League final, the way they did in the past, but there’d be more parity. It’d fix most of the game’s problems. Unfortunately, it wouldn’t flush the turds that are Newcastle, PSG, and Manchester City, but it’d still be a big step forward.

Scotland's Time to Shine

Quick side-note: perhaps I’m overrating past footballers a bit. People do it all the time, venerating the likes of Paolo Rossi, George Best, and Zinedine Zidane, as if they’re footballing paragons. They cleverly brush aside the fact that Rossi was finished at the age of 29, Best at 26, and that Zidane was often criticised for his inconsistency. Also, both Juventus and Real Madrid declined after a couple of years of the Zidane Experience, and Rossi’s career almost ended in disgrace at the age of 23, when he was banned for alleged match-fixing. The likes of Cruyff, Bobby Moore, Paul McGrath, etc. also had their downsides, which nobody seems to mention. Maybe this revisionism has rubbed off on me.

Zidane being sent off in France's match against Saudi Arabia, 1998. Had France not won the competition, maybe we'd view Zidane as a hot-headed bottler. But, Zidane was a serial winner, so people overlooked his flaws.

It hasn’t. I’m right. If you watch clips/matches of the greats, you’ll instantly notice their sublime technical ability, vision, and bottle. These days, very few footballers shape up. You rarely see footballers taken on 5 players in a European Cup final, the way Best did in 1968 (at the age of 22!). I can only think of Mbappe, Haaland, Modric, and Kroos. Every other footballer is the same: incredible stamina, strength, and speed, but poor technical ability. They’re especially rife in the Premier League.

This is probably a weird observation to many. Some believed that British footballers pre-1992 were talentless, brainless workhorses, who were only capable of smashing into challenges. But, the likes of Norman Whiteside, Bryan Robson, Graeme Souness, David Rocastle, and John Robertson would put many modern players to shame. A 27-year old John Robertson would make Adama Traoré like silly. His crossing and dribbling, as well as the general application of his talents, were levels above.

Regardless, one player spent his entire career at Nottingham Forest and Derby County, earning a pittance. Meanwhile, the other used his massive biceps to blag his way into a €100k/week deal at Barcelona. Another non-entity, Mykhailo Mudryk, swindled Chelsea with his neck tattoos and good looks. It’s a really, really weird climate. With the way the game’s gone, I reckon Christian McCaffrey could bag a move to Barcelona. Franz Mesmer could also snag a spot on a top club’s medical team. Probably City’s, given Guardiola’s history of dubious medical practices.

If Adama can bag a move to Barcelona, surely Christian McCaffrey could do the same? They're both built the same, and McCaffrey could probably cross the ball better.

Anyway, as I said earlier, a lot of sides are poor, even the (supposed) top ones. Very little separates Scotland from them. This is just based on player quality, too. I also think managerial quality has gone down in recent years. I remember someone saying—I think it was Arsene Wenger—that a great manager can make a squad perform 10% better than the sum of its parts, whereas a crap one can make it perform 30% worse.

I think most managers lean towards the latter. Don’t believe me? England’s and Portugal’s fanbases want their respective managers out. Klopp has retired, and Guardiola’s (hopefully) on his way out at City. Like A$AP Rocky said on Potato Salad, “I find it hard to find actual talent.” There’s little managerial quality left; you don’t see any Ron Revies, Walter Smiths, Alex Fergusons, or Ron Saunderses in the game, and, as a result, the top clubs are suffering. Yesterday, I saw that Bayern Munich were courting Ten Hag, Julen Lopetegui, and Ralf Rangnick for their managerial role. Christ. I reckon David Moyes would do a better job than all three.

Scotland's Key Players

In this section, I’ll be focusing on 3 footballers: McTominay, Robertson, and McGinn. These are Scotland’s best players, and I think they’ll do damage this summer.

This won’t be a tactical analysis; moreso, a description of the players’ qualities. I’ll leave the formation, positioning, and tactical roles to Steve Clarke. Besides, I think you’d get more from an overview of the players. I think football is similar to a greedy algorithm; if your players consistently make the best (or second best) decision 98% of the time, your team will likely win. I think it is that simple.

As Bill Shankly once said, just keep the ball on the ground, and pass it to an open teammate. Sometimes simple is better. Anyway:

Scott McTominay

Scott McTominay plays for Manchester United. He is lanky, but determined. He’s a midfielder by trade, but, when United are chasing a goal, he plays up front. As a player, he has many redeeming qualities: he’s got bottle, pace, strength, and solid defensive instinct. Surprisingly, he’s also got great offensive instinct. He times his runs well, akin to a young Dele Alli, and is a good finisher. All-in-all, he’s kinda like a jack-of-all-trades, but a master-of-none.

McTominay putting in a challenge that'd make Ollie Chessum proud.

Sometimes, his concentration lacks. When United lost 2-1 to Forest, he was majorly at fault for both goals, not paying attention to the lad behind him. Many also accuse him of hiding from the ball, i.e. not making himself open for passes. These are all fair criticisms, but I think they’re unfair. McTominay’s not the perfect footballer, but he gives it a go and has enough technical ability to create a role for himself at England’s biggest club. That’s not easy to do. Past “superstars”, like Di María, Pogba, and Verón, have failed.

The reason McTominay has succeeded at United, whilst others have failed, is because he has bottle and an incredible attitude. He can’t coast on talent alone, like Pogba did for 6 years, so his effort has to be spot-on 100% of the time. His effort is why every United manager has loved him: Ten Hag, Solskjaer, and even José Mourinho. Mourinho, reputed to be a chequebook manager with a disinclination towards youth, gave McTominay a consistent role in the squad. He hasn’t done that with many other young lads. In 2018, at the club’s end-of-season party, Mourinho even created an award, Manager's Player of the Year, to recognise McTominay’s efforts. So, he’s clearly a good footballer.

Further, he shows up in big games, having put in great performances in matches against Arsenal, Liverpool, and Manchester City. Even for Scotland, he scored a last-minute winner against Israel in World Cup qualifiers and put Spain away with a brace. Moreover, in United’s infamous 2021 Europa League final against Villarreal, he was the best player on the pitch. He didn’t hide; he pinged the ball cross-field, snuffed out Villarreal’s chances, and made himself available to his teammates. Unfortunately, United lost, but he was incredible.

Meanwhile, Paul Pogba, his flash midfield partner, was an absolute disgrace. He made poor decisions and had little-to-no impact on the game. He was at fault for Villarreal’s goal, as their free-kick came off the back of one of his many poor decisions. He had ages on the ball and plenty teammates open, but he picked the worst possible passing avenue and forced his teammate to make the foul. Poor.

He didn't want to play.

Anyway, McTominay’s class.

Andy Robertson

Andy Robertson is Liverpool’s left-back, and he’s world-class. He has humble origins. As a young lad, after being released from Celtic, he worked the tills at Marks & Spencer. After, Third Division Queen’s Park took a chance on him, and he impressed. After one season, Dundee United picked him up, where he also impressed. Again, after one season, Hull City signed him for a meagre £2.85 million. He wasn’t a consistent starter, and Hull got relegated in 2015. However, Robertson established himself as a star in the Championship and helped get Hull promoted. Unfortunately, the season after, Hull got relegated again, despite having him, Harry Maguire, Kamil Grosicki, and other quality footballers.

Two great footballers.

After Hull’s second relegation, both Robertson and Maguire were picked up by Liverpool and Leicester, respectively. Much like at Hull, Robertson had a slow start at Liverpool, only making 30 appearances in his first season. However, the year Liverpool won the Champions League, Robertson had a blinder. He made 48 appearances and established himself as a mainstay in Klopp’s team, endearing himself to the fans with his incredible work ethic and attitude. However, beyond the intangibles, he’s a talented footballer, with incredible crossing ability and decision-making, albeit not on the level of his Trent Alexander-Arnold, his right-sided counterpart.

As Scotland often play a back 3, Robertson plays as a left wing-back. He doesn’t have as much impact for Scotland as he does for Liverpool. Obviously a world-class talent, but he has struggled to elevate his teammates to his level. It’s easy playing one-twos with Wijnaldum and Mane, or putting in crosses for Van Dijk and Firmino, but replicating that impact with the likes of Oliver McBurnie and Ryan Christie is a different story. However, I back him to make a difference this tournament. Scotland have their strongest squad in decades, with Lyndon Dykes, Che Adams, and Jacob Brown offering more reliable outlets. This summer, he’ll do great.

John McGinn

Now, John McGinn is class. He has played for Aston Villa the last 6 years, and he’s been excellent. In his first season at Villa, 2018-19, he helped them get promoted and was named the club’s Player of the Season. Since then, Villa have survived in the Premier League and, barring the Gerrard blip, have gotten better each season. This season, they’ll likely finish 4th place and play Champions League football for the first time in ages. I think the last time they’d played in the European Cup was in 1983, the season after they’d won it. He has played a huge role in bringing Villa back to those heights.

McGinn doing his signature celebration.

Unlike McTominay, McGinn is naturally gifted, with great vision, passing, and dribbling. A bit of a silly comparison, but he’s akin to Mateo Kovačić, with better passing ability. His engine is incredible, too. All of these qualities have made him the first name on both Scotland’s and Villa’s team sheets. This chap is really, really good, and I genuinely mean that.

Group

Scotland’s group consists of Hungary, Germany, and Switzerland. If this were 1954, then Scotland would be in huge trouble. Puskás, Hidegkuti, and Kocsis would demolish the Scots, and Fritz Walter would score a hat-trick of outside-the-foot curlers. The Scots would exit the tournament in disarray.

Hungary's 1954 side. Arguably the best international side of all time.

However, this is 2024. The Hungarian National Team has fallen off since the Hungarian Revolution of 1956. I reckon Scotland will batter them. They have one good footballer, Dominik Szoboszlai, a couple of decent ones (e.g. Willi Orban and Peter Gulacsi), and the rest are journeymen. Although they did the double over England in 2022, they’ve struggled against the likes of Luxembourg, Lithuania, and Bulgaria in recent times. They’re no pushovers, but they’re not world-beaters, either, and they haven’t been for a while.

Germany, on the other hand, are footballing royalty. They have won the World Cup four times and the European Championship three times. The likes of Gerd Müller, Franz Beckenbauer, Lothar Matthäus, Karl-Heinz Rummenigge, Günter Netzer, and Bernd Schuster are amongst the greats, and Bundesliga sides have regularly dominated European competition.

In 2024, Germany are still class. Xabi Alonso’s Bayer Leverkusen have established themselves as a top side, erasing their Neverkusen monicker. Bayern have lost Lewandowski and are struggling in the Bundesliga, but still have a chance of winning the Champions League. The national team has Kroos, Müller, Gündogan, and Neuer—all world-class footballers—but it seems their time is up. They fell at the first hurdle in both 2014 and 2018 and were unable to get past England in 2021. Having serial winners in the squad always boosts your chances, but their lacklustre performances in three of the last four major tournaments don't inspire confidence.

Germany are a fantastic international side, arguably the best ever, but they've been poor as of late.

Switzerland and Scotland are similar, though Scotland are bigger bottlers. Both sides haven’t gone deep into tournaments before, but Switzerland have had less quality. In recent times, they’ve churned out Granit Xhaka, Xherdan Shaqiri, Yann Sommer, Manuel Akanji, and Stephan Lichsteiner—all of whom are class. Compared to Scotland’s recent output—Scott McTominay, Andy Robertson, and John McGinn—and it’s clear that the two countries are on similar pegging.

However, in terms of historic quality, it’s a wash. Stéphane Chapuisat is Switzerland’s best ever player, and, to be fair, he was class. But, he’s one of the few world-class players that Switzerland have produced. As for Scotland, it’s a long list: Bremner, Law, Souness, Dalglish, Hansen, Robertson, Mackay, etc. Obviously, current quality is all that matters, but the Scots have a CV, whereas the Swiss don’t.

All in all, I think Scotland draw 1-1 with Germany, and Scotland beat Switzerland 2-1 and Hungary 3-0. I back the Scots to top the group; I think Germany will draw against Switzerland.
